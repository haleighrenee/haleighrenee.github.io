Introduction

When I made the decision to pursue a career in teaching, I did so because of the safety and relationships I found in the classroom as a student. Without many of the incredible educators who inspired me, I am unsure of where I would be today. Thus, I knew from the conception of this career plan that the most important part of teaching for me would be the connections I make with my students. My teaching philosophy is grounded in two major facets: rhetorical actions and student relationships.

Rhetorical Value & Relationships

In order to teach effectively, I find that it is pertinent to understand one’s own rhetorical situation. All of my classroom decisions, whether I actively realize it or not, stem from a rhetorical choice that I am making for myself and for my students. Because teaching is rhetorical in nature, educators must recognize their own purpose, audience, and appeal to students. Every class I encounter throughout the day has a totally different makeup of students; therefore, my actions and interactions must always be rhetorically effective for the audience to which I am presenting. I intend to find a way to effectively present myself to every class of students I happen to be assigned—if I notice a class does not respond well to how I present information and ideas, then I know to consider how to better reach next time I see them. Moreover, I find that in order for students to “buy-in” to my class, they must perceive me as having the credibility and capability to teach them well while also trusting me. I strive to be clear in my expectations and requirements so students can trust that my requests of them are purposeful and intentional. I find this also helps to build relationships with students, as my openness with them establishes a clear trusting relationship from the start of each year.

While I work to situate my content presentation with a clear rhetorical purpose, I am also consistently working on building strong relationships with my students. I tell my students often that my goal for them is to learn to communicate more effectively—even if they do not perfect their writing skills in their time with me, my hope is that they better understand how to express ideas. In order to build this experience for students, though, they have to feel safe to express themselves in my classroom. I never want students to listen to me simply because they fear me. My job is not to scare students into submission, and I always work toward building an environment of safety and acceptance of all students. I cannot expect a student to learn anything from me if their well-being is compromised. This is truly why I put relationship building at the top of my priorities at the start of each year. If I know students well enough, then I can sense when they are having an off day. Strong relationships also help me determine whether students are growing. Many students produce brilliant ideas but struggle with how to showcase their thoughts, so in building relationships with them I know how to build assignments with a choice for students to present their learning how they are comfortable; giving a bit of choice and freedom also encourages student engagement.

After establishing strong relationships with students, I find that their level of engagement also improves. English is, unfortunately, not all students’ favorite subject. Often, they enter my classroom dejected from past struggles with reading and writing and they are prepared to face defeat again. I never want students to feel stuck in their preconceived ideas of what English class looks like. Returning to my earlier assertion that my main goal is improving communication, I try to focus students on their growth. Instead of moving from one assignment to the next with no clarification, we take time for reflecting and learning what we’ve improved upon and what might need work in the future. This helps students, I believe, maintain engagement because they are less fearful of failing or embarrassing themselves. I often remind students that English is easier than they might realize because there are no wrong answers as long as they support their ideas. This low-pressure nudge tends to help them continue wanting to learn rather than checking out when they dislike a class.

Accountability & Safety

Although my priority is building relationships, my students know that I hold them to a high standard for active learning and strong writing. While I certainly allow what others might see as chaos (active collaboration and communication), my students know what I expect of them. I encourage “bad” writing: in the early stages of starting a novel or unit, I have my students write their thoughts and ideas before there is any true plan for where that writing may go. Sometimes we revisit it, but sometimes it just serves as a process for getting them to continue developing thinking and communication skills. I also incorporate rhetoric in the content I teach my students for this reason. It helps guide them to a stronger level of understanding of their own writing while also showing them why I make the choices I do as their leader. Once they understand the basis of rhetoric, they start to make better assumptions in regard to what they read. I believe when my students begin to make intentional choices in their writing, they begin to hold themselves to a higher standard and put forth the utmost effort.

I prioritize rhetoric and relationships because I value students understanding the why behind what I ask them to do. I look to my own learning, in which I most valued teachers with a clear purpose for teaching certain content, and try to emulate what led to my own success. I learned the best from those teachers who gave me respect and created an environment of safety, and I want my students to experience something similar. My hope is that every single student who walks through my door feels safe enough to come to me for help while also learning to grow their communication skills. In an era of ever-rising mental health issues, I do not want any student to feel under-valued or as though they are part of an assembly line of cookie-cutter kids meant to sit and get information and move on to the next part of life. I value their brilliance and silliness, and I hope every student feels safe to be themselves when they are in my classroom.
